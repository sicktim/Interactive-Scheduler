Flight Sciences: Flying Qualities Phase 
The flying qualities phase is designed to introduce the student to modern flying qualities academics, 
resources, vocabulary, skills and test approaches such that the graduate will be able to thoroughly and 
systematically evaluate the flying qualities of any air vehicle, platform or system. Specifically, the student 
will learn how dynamics of air vehicles and operators interact in flight. The flying qualities Phase is designed 
to explore aerodynamics, flight mechanics, controls and structures individually and their interactions with the 
air vehicle as a system. Further, the flying qualities Phase will discuss aircraft flight envelopes and how to 
safely manage technical and safety risk as the flight envelope is opened and explored for testing and 
operational use. Upon graduation, the student will have both an academic as well as an intuitive and practical 
understand of these topics and will have the necessary tools, references, contacts and or knowledge to safely 
and efficiently execute a flying qualities evaluation.

Additional Information 
Homework 
As a general rule, homework assignments will be given to broaden the students’ understanding of a topic 
covered in block. The homework assignment may extend beyond that material covered in class to cover 
additional material found in your text. The homework assignment is meant to be a learning exercise, and as 
such normally can be done in a group setting, instructor dependent, so long as each person turns in his/her 
own work. Copying of material turned in is not allowed in any form, unless specifically stated by the 
instructor. 
FQ Reference Cards 
A model aircraft and a FQ formula reference card will be provided at the beginning of the FQ Phase. Both 
reference materials will be the only set provided. They will be allowed for use on any FQ quiz, test, exam, 
homework, or other activity in the Phase. During the FQ 8000 course a second reference card (Cooper 
Harper Rating Scale and Pilot Induced Oscillation Tendency Classification scale) will be provided. This card 
will also be allowed as a reference during any quiz, test, exam, homework, or other activity in the FQ Phase. 
Quizzes and Tests 
Tests are completed without the aid of another student. Tests are designed to assess the students’ 
knowledge and not to teach a new concept. Specifically, testing will be administered to determine if the 
students’ are meeting the learning objectives for a given event. During flying qualities tests, the model aircraft 
and flying qualities reference cards are allowed. Other reference materials (including on-line references) may 
be allowed during a specific test and will be provided by the instructor. 
Reports 
The culmination of most flight tests are the reports that are used to disseminate the information to the rest 
of the test community. The reports will often require the student to take data from multiple flight tests and 
support conclusions and recommendations with analysis. All major report products in the FQ Phase will be 
done as a group report. Depending on the event, the reporting products may be an informal oral report, an 
informal written report, a formal written report, a formal oral report, or a combination thereof. 
Curriculum Organization and Learning Objectives 
The flying qualities curriculum is organized topically into 4 major courses. Within each course is a set of 
events (classes, labs, or flight tests). The major courses are: 6000 Aircraft Flight Mechanics, 7000 Flight 
Control Systems, 8000 Handling Qualities Evaluation, and 9000 Envelope Expansion.  
Learning in each event may be evaluated by assigned homework, a quiz, a test report (oral or written), or a 
direct evaluation of your performance during that event. A block diagram showing how the courses relate to 
an aircraft/pilot system can be found in Figure 19. The following section will present a brief description of 
each course and its top-level learning objectives. Detailed learning objectives will be provided at each 
curriculum event. An overview of the curriculum can be seen in Figure 20.

FQ 6000 Aircraft Flight Mechanics 
(3 Semester Credit Hours) 
The 6000-level course gives the student an understanding of aircraft rigid body motion. Students will 
learn how to compare various aircraft and predict their dynamics based on an understanding of the aircraft 
equations of motion and analysis techniques. 
FQ 6100 Aircraft Equations of Motion  
Terminal Learning Objectives: 
Upon completion of this Module, students will be able to: 
TLO 1. Describe the fundamental principles governing the rigid-body equations of motion for aircraft. 
TLO 2. Explain the differences between body-axis, stability-axis, and wind-axis reference frames in 
aircraft dynamics. 
TLO 3. Apply the six-degree-of-freedom equations of motion to determine aircraft translational and 
rotational motion. 
TLO 4. Characterize the effects of simplifying assumptions on the nonlinear and linearized equations 
of motion. 
TLO 5. Compute aircraft dynamic responses using numerical methods to solve the equations of 
motion. 
TLO 6. Discuss modern methods of interpreting equations of motion, such as uncertainty quantification 
and sensitivity analysis. 
 
FQ 6101A Aircraft Equations of Motion Lecture 
FQ 6102E Aircraft Equations of Motion Exam 
FQ 6103Z Aircraft Equations of Motion Debrief

The 7000-level course provides the student with an understanding of aircraft linear control systems as

Terminal Learning Objectives:

FQ 7102E Flight Control Systems Exam

Feedback flight control systems are introduced along with motivation for transition from mechanical to 
automatic flight control systems. Linear analysis techniques are presented with applications to aircraft flight 
controls. These fundamental concepts will be used throughout the 7000-level course. Feedback flight control 
system implementation is introduced to demonstrate how augmented control systems are used to enhance air 
vehicle stability and performance. Examples of classical proportional, integral, derivative and LQR (linear

design and analysis techniques are then applied to aircraft. Considerations for modern aircraft with relevant 
examples are given as well as issues associated with flight testing control systems. Finally, an introduction to 
digital control and other analysis planes are discussed. 
Prerequisites:
