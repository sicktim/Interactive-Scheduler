Mission Systems Phase 
The Mission Systems Phase is designed to provide a foundation for testers in key aspects of military 
mission systems development and test. This foundation consists of a review of the physics of and the 
functionality provided by specific blocks of mission systems, and test techniques applicable to those 
systems. 
The Mission Systems Phase consists of academic lectures, laboratories, simulators, and flights 
introducing manned and unmanned aircraft and spacecraft mission systems including communications, 
navigation, RADAR, multi-spectral optics, datalinks, weapons, electronic warfare, autonomy, and cyber 
susceptibility test design methodologies among others. Mission systems are examined from both an 
individual and integrated perspective. Students receive hands-on training in laboratory and mission 
systems simulators as well as operate mission hardware in ground test, Hardware-In-The-Loop (HITL) 
facilities and in air vehicles. Students may receive flights in the F-16, F-15E, MQ-9, as well as other 
advanced systems based on resource availability.

prefix. Each student at TPS has access to the five texts to be used. They should be considered the primary

This block will cover data-driven testing for mission systems, including a broad scope of topics that 
include data science, uncertainty quantification, test & evaluation, statistical hypothesis testing, and 
decision making. Topics covered in the 1-week block include exploratory data analysis, data 
visualization, structured vs unstructured data, streaming vs. post-mission data, meta-data tagging, and

performance and predictive modelling, validation and verification in data-driven test, statistical 
comparisons, Experimental design, adversarial machine learning & resilience strategies, and software for 
T&E of data-driven mission systems. Additionally, the block will cover probabilistic reasoning, 
reinforcement learning, autonomous control with RL (model-free and model-based), ethical 
considerations, data and decision fusion techniques, and considerations for tactical vs. operational vs. 
strategic environments.

discussion of the errors and analysis of large sets of navigation data.

instructor in the form of a notional navigation flight test program with specific requirements and

SY 7110A Computer Software and Networks Lecture 
SY 7111L Computer Software and Networks Lab 
SY 7112A SAP/SCI Cyber Threats Lecture (US Only) 
This is a 3-hour academic overview of the types of documentation and specifications common in 
software and networks mission systems testing. The MIL-STD-1760 interface and MIL-STD-1553 data 
bus protocol is introduced as well as other military and commercial data busses. This block introduces the 
various software development and test methodologies used by defense industry partners and the DoD for 
aircraft, space and cyber systems, and how requirements definition, development, and test approaches 
differ between legacy and more modern development frameworks. It focuses on how to create a software 
test plan that meets specification, performance, maintenance, and quality requirements while effectively 
managing the tradeoffs between risk, quality, and cost. Finally, the block introduces the DevSecOps 
cycle, Open Mission Systems fundamentals, and continuous integration and continuous development 
concepts as applied to software testing of aircraft and spacecraft systems. This block also discusses 
vehicles as a system of systems, then decomposes individual systems using the design process down to 
specific mission systems, mission essential functionality, and cyber-physical systems technologies. This 
provides a context necessary to understand how information flow within cyber-physical systems and 
physical technology shapes integrated avionics/regression testing as well as cyber vulnerability analysis.  
These labs reinforce academic knowledge acquired in the lecture with a 1-hour lab conducted with 4 
students at a time. Students can manipulate code on a stand-alone computer to illustrate vulnerabilities 
such as buffer overflows and race conditions. They are also exposed to automated testing using CI/CD 
pipelines. Additionally, students demonstrate the performance differences between programming 
languages (compiled and interpreted), virtualization technologies (virtual machines and containers), 
Internet Protocol (IP) network data transfers (TCP and UDP), and cryptographic algorithms (AES and 
RSA) to better internalize the tradeoffs and promise of the cutting-edge computer science currently being 
used in modern mission systems architectures. 
 
Prerequisites: 
SY 5101A Mission Systems Fundamentals Lecture
